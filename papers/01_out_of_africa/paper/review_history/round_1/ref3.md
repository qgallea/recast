## Referee 3 Report: Robustness and Replication Integrity
**Round:** 1
**Overall verdict:** Major concerns

---

### Blocking issues

- **Two replication failures exceed stated tolerance thresholds.** The replication check reports a worst-case relative deviation of 33.5 % (`replication_check.json`: `worst_rel_diff_pct: 33.46`). Table 1 col 4 (OLS, 21-country, historical) deviates 29.4 % and Table 2 col 5 (2SLS IV, 21-country, historical) deviates 33.5 %. Even under the relaxed 10 % threshold for bootstrap/IV specifications, both figures fail. The pipeline's own diagnostic flags these as blocking (`diagnostics_flags.json`: `blocking_issues: ["replication_pass", ...]`). The paper's narrative explanation (small-$n$ fragility, unavailability of Conley spatial SEs) is plausible but unverified in the code output; no quantitative sensitivity analysis is provided to support the claim that the deviation is entirely attributable to SE-type differences rather than to a data-version mismatch or filter discrepancy.

- **RF nuisance model for the treatment is degenerate.** `dml_results.json` records `nuisance_r2_treatment: -127810.35` for Random Forest, a wildly negative $R^2$ that signals complete failure of the treatment nuisance. This renders the RF-PLIV estimate ($\hat\theta = -286.33$, SE $= 378.63$, 95 % CI $[-1251.76,\, 455.77]$, $p = 0.45$) scientifically unusable. The paper correctly identifies LassoCV as the preferred learner, but the RF estimate is still presented in the DML comparison table without a clear visual or textual flag marking it as degenerate, which could mislead readers.

### Major issues

- **Replication failures confined to 21-country sample — but the explanation is qualitative, not quantitative.** The paper states that "small sample ($N = 21$) makes the coefficient highly sensitive to data-version differences and the precise set of countries passing the `cleanlim` filter." However, no sensitivity table is provided showing how the coefficient changes as countries are added or removed, and no documentation of which 21 countries are in or out of the replication sample is given. Given that the original paper uses Conley (1999) spatial SEs that "cannot be exactly reproduced," the paper should at minimum report what SE type was actually used in the replication and quantify the SE gap separately from the coefficient gap.

- **Forest plot shows only four rows (two OLS, two DML-PLIV) against a paper that targets six specifications.** The plot title reads "OLS vs DML-PLIV — Effect of Genetic Diversity on Log GDP per Capita (2000)." It omits the two historical-outcome OLS specifications (Tables 1 and 3) and the IV specification (Table 2) that are explicitly part of the replication exercise. Figure 1 caption in the paper mentions "OLS (Tables 1, 3, 6, 7), 2SLS (Table 2), and DML-PLIV (LassoCV)," but the actual image contains only two OLS rows (Table 6, $n = 143$; Table 7, $n = 106$), the degenerate RF estimate, and the Lasso estimate. The historical OLS and 2SLS rows are missing from the forest plot, creating a discrepancy between the caption claim and the visual output.

- **N = 148 for DML versus N = 143 / 109 for OLS baselines — not explained.** `dml_results.json` records $N = 148$ for both DML-PLIV learners. The OLS baselines use $N = 143$ (Table 6) and $N = 109$ (Table 7). The paper does not explain why the DML sample is 5 observations larger than the nearest OLS baseline. If the DML run uses a different variable set (e.g., drops the quadratic term and therefore retains observations with missing `pdiv_aa_sqr`), this should be stated explicitly. Sample inconsistency across otherwise-comparable estimates is a robustness concern.

- **DML CI coverage warning is under-treated.** `diagnostics_flags.json` flags `dml_ci_coverage` as a WARNING, noting that the published OLS coefficient of 225.44 lies outside the LassoCV 95 % CI of $[-34.27,\,-12.11]$. The paper discusses the sign change at length but does not directly address CI non-overlap. The two CIs — OLS and DML — are not comparable at face value because they estimate different estimands (quadratic OLS slope vs. linear LATE), but this needs to be stated more precisely in the paper, and the diagnostics table note is too terse ("Published coef. outside DML CI").

### Minor issues

- **Table 1 col 4 SE type mismatch.** `paper_spec.json` records "Heteroskedasticity-robust SE" for the 21-country limited-sample specification, while Section 2.1 of the paper states that "historical small-sample regressions in the original paper also use Conley (1999) spatial standard errors." The two descriptions are inconsistent. If the replication used HC robust SEs but the original used Conley SEs, this should be noted in the replication table as a known source of divergence, not only in the main text.

- **`replication_check.json` reports `"pass": false` with summary "4/6 specs replicated within 5%."** The 5 % threshold is stated for pure OLS; the paper uses a 10 % threshold for bootstrap/IV. Under the 10 % threshold, the historical Table 3 col 5 (no FE) and Table 3 col 6 (with FE) presumably pass, but the check file applies a flat 5 % cutoff. It is unclear whether the pipeline's pass/fail logic properly implements the two-tier threshold (5 % OLS / 10 % bootstrap-IV) described in the paper's text and the diagnostics table.

- **No robustness checks for the DML specification.** The paper offers no sensitivity analysis of the preferred $\hat\theta = -23.19$ to: (a) alternative control sets (e.g., dropping continent fixed effects); (b) alternative fold structures ($K = 3$ or $K = 10$); (c) trimming the RF result and relying on a single-learner ensemble. The conclusion acknowledges "richer instrument sets or ensemble learners" as future work, but a minimal within-paper robustness check (e.g., varying $K$) is standard practice and feasible with the existing data.

- **Forest plot x-axis range is driven by the degenerate RF estimate.** The RF CI extends from approximately $-1252$ to $+456$, forcing the x-axis to span $[-1250, +500]$. This extreme scale compresses the LassoCV CI ($[-34, -12]$) to a visually indistinguishable point near zero, making it impossible to read the preferred estimate's confidence interval from the figure. The RF row should either be excluded from the plot (with a note) or plotted on a separate panel, so that the non-degenerate estimates are legible.

### Comments to the authors

**Replication fidelity.** Four of six specifications pass numerical replication, which is a reasonable outcome for a cross-decade replication using publicly available data. However, the two failures are not minor: the 21-country historical sample is the primary evidence base for the paper's IV identification exercise (Table 2), and a 33.5 % deviation on the IV coefficient is large enough to undermine confidence in the exact numerical claims. The paper's explanation is credible in direction — small-$n$ sensitivity and Conley SE unavailability — but it needs to be backed by quantitative evidence. At minimum, the authors should report: (i) the list of 21 countries in the replication sample versus the original, (ii) the OLS/HC coefficient alongside the Conley-SE-adjusted figure if that can be reconstructed, and (iii) a brief simulation or jackknife showing how the coefficient shifts when one country is dropped, to quantify the fragility claim.

**DML sign change and CI non-overlap.** The negative LassoCV estimate of $-23.19$ (95 % CI: $[-34.27, -12.11]$) is sharply at odds with the OLS linear coefficient of 203–281. The paper's two-point explanation — linearisation of a quadratic treatment and LATE weighting — is intellectually sound and the paper deserves credit for presenting it clearly. Nonetheless, the CI non-overlap flag in the diagnostics warrants explicit treatment: readers should be told plainly that the DML and OLS estimates are not testing the same hypothesis (quadratic ATE vs. linear LATE for complier countries at the instrument's margin), and that the forest plot is therefore not a direct comparison of competing estimates of the same parameter. The current caption is ambiguous on this point.

**Forest plot presentation.** The forest plot as rendered has two significant presentational defects. First, the RF estimate's enormous CI ($[-1252, +456]$) forces an x-axis scale that renders the LassoCV CI ($[-34, -12]$) unreadable — the preferred estimate appears as a single point with no visible whiskers. Second, the caption promises historical OLS (Tables 1, 3) and 2SLS (Table 2) rows, but the image shows only contemporary OLS rows (Tables 6, 7). This is either a code bug (rows not plotted) or a caption error. Either way it must be corrected: either add the missing rows (which would provide the visual replication check the forest plot is designed to deliver) or correct the caption and explain the omission.

**Missing robustness checks.** The paper reports a single DML specification with a single preferred learner. No within-paper sensitivity analysis is offered for $\hat\theta = -23.19$ — not to the number of folds, not to the control set, not to dropping the continent fixed effects. Given that the RF learner fails completely (nuisance $R^2_D = -127\,810$), the precision and robustness of the LassoCV result rest on a single implementation choice. The revision should add at least one alternative specification (e.g., $K = 3$ folds, or dropping continent FEs) to give readers confidence that $-23.19$ is not an artifact of the cross-fitting hyperparameters. The sample-size discrepancy ($N = 148$ for DML vs. $N = 143$ for the closest OLS baseline) should also be resolved and documented.
