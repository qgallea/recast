### Blocking issues (require re-running a notebook)

| # | Issue | Raised by | Notebook to fix | Specific action |
|---|-------|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| 1 | **Quadratic treatment omitted from PLIV / estimand mismatch.** The PLIV is specified with only the linear term `pdiv_aa` as the treatment vector. The paper's causal claim rests on a quadratic (hump-shaped) relationship, so the linearised PLIV recovers a different estimand (the slope of a concave function above its mode, which is negative by construction at the sample mean of ~0.72) rather than a genuine extension of the original quadratic ATE. The sign flip to −23.19 is a predictable artefact of this misspecification. **Fix (choose one):** (a) Re-specify the PLIV to include both `pdiv_aa` and `pdiv_aa_sqr` as the treatment vector so the estimand is comparable to the original; or (b) explicitly reframe the DML section as estimating a distinct linear LATE at the instrument-weighted mean, state it is a *different* estimand from the quadratic ATE, and remove all direct coefficient comparisons between the DML estimate and the original quadratic OLS coefficient. | R1, R3 (partially) | `04_dml_extension` | See fix options above; option (a) requires re-specification and re-execution of Stage 4. |
| 2 | **Degenerate RF treatment nuisance (R² = −127,810).** The Random Forest `ml_m` nuisance produces `nuisance_r2_treatment = −127,810.35` in `dml_results.json`. This is a computational pathology — almost certainly caused by misalignment between the averaged prediction array (`obj_rf.predictions['ml_m'].mean(axis=1)`) and `dml_data.d` when computing the R² score. The RF-PLIV coefficient (−286.33, SE = 378.63) is therefore scientifically unusable. While the paper correctly prefers LassoCV, the degenerate R² is logged in `nuisance_scores`, misleads readers, and must be corrected or explicitly invalidated in the JSON output and any table that displays it. The forest plot x-axis is also distorted by the RF CI (approximately −1252 to +456), rendering the preferred LassoCV CI visually unreadable. | R1, R2, R3 | `04_dml_extension` | Debug the R² computation for the RF treatment nuisance (fix index alignment or recompute correctly); update `dml_results.json`; either remove the RF row from the forest plot or plot it on a separate panel; re-execute Stage 4. |
| 3 | **No nuisance R² computed or stored for the preferred LassoCV learner.** The `dml_results.json` `nuisance_scores` field contains only RF values. The LassoCV fit cell (`cell id 087e4487`) does not compute or save nuisance R² for either the outcome or treatment equation. Without these values it is impossible to assess whether the preferred learner achieves adequate fit. The treatment RMSE (~5.70–5.75) is high relative to the scale of `pdiv_aa` and must be checked. | R2 | `04_dml_extension` | Add post-fit R² computation for both `ml_l` and `ml_m` nuisances under LassoCV; store in `dml_results.json`; re-execute Stage 4. |

### Major issues (prose or table edits only)

| # | Issue | Raised by | Action |
|---|-------|-----------|--------|
| 1 | **Estimand definition in Section 3.1 is imprecise.** The paper describes the PLIV with scalar linear treatment but the stated estimand in `paper_spec.json` is the quadratic ATE. Section 3.1 must explicitly define what estimand the PLIV recovers (e.g., slope at the instrument-weighted mean), distinguish it from the quadratic ATE, and justify why it is of scientific interest. This is required regardless of which fix path is chosen for Blocking Issue #1. | R1 | Prose edit to Section 3.1. |
| 2 | **Exclusion restriction threats unaddressed.** Section 2.1 asserts the exclusion restriction without evaluating threats. Migratory distance from Addis Ababa correlates with Neolithic transition timing, disease ecology, and proximity to Eurasian trade networks — channels not fully absorbed by included controls. The paper must acknowledge these threats and cite the original paper's or subsequent literature's responses (e.g., Spolaore and Wacziarg 2013). | R1 | Prose edit to Section 2.1. |
| 3 | **First-stage F-statistic not reported.** The 2SLS specification (Table 2, col 5) uses `mdist_addis` as instrument but no first-stage F-statistic is reported anywhere. For IV validity the Staiger-Stock rule of thumb requires F > 10. This figure should be computed and reported for both the 21-country and 145-country samples. The omission is particularly consequential given the 33.5% deviation in the 2SLS replication. | R1 | Compute and add first-stage F to the replication results table or a dedicated IV diagnostics table. (If not already computed in a notebook, this is a code edit; however the figure can also be extracted from existing Stata/Python output — if already available in `results/` JSON files, a prose/table edit suffices.) |
| 4 | **Replication failures for 21-country sample are explained qualitatively, not quantitatively.** Table 1 col 4 (OLS, 21-country, historical) deviates 29.4% and Table 2 col 5 (2SLS, 21-country, historical) deviates 33.5%, both exceeding the stated thresholds. The narrative explanation (small-n fragility, Conley SE unavailability) is plausible but unverified. The paper must provide: (i) the list of 21 countries in the replication sample versus the original; (ii) the SE type actually used in the replication versus the original Conley SE; and (iii) at minimum a brief jackknife or leave-one-out illustration quantifying coefficient fragility for the 21-country sample. | R1, R3 | Prose edit to Section 2.2; add a small supplementary table or footnote with the country list and jackknife results. |

Historical OLS and 2SLS rows are absent. This is either a code bug (rows not plotted) or a caption error and must be corrected. | R3 | Edit the forest plot generation code in `04_dml_extension` or `06_report` to add the missing rows, OR correct the caption and explain the omission. If rows are added, Stage 4 / Stage 6 re-run is needed; if caption-only fix, this is a prose edit. |

The paper discusses the sign change but does not state plainly that the DML and OLS estimates are not testing the same hypothesis. Readers must be told explicitly that the forest plot is not a direct comparison of competing estimates of the same parameter (quadratic ATE vs. linear LATE for complier countries). | R2, R3 | Prose edit to Section 3.3 and forest plot caption. |

For N = 148 with 8 covariates, additional learner diversity is both feasible and important for assessing robustness of nuisance fit. ### Referee disagreements

No outright contradictions between referees were identified. All three referees reached the same overall verdict (major concerns) and agreed on the two central blocking pathologies (degenerate RF nuisance and linearised PLIV estimand mismatch). R2 raised the missing LassoCV nuisance R² as a separate blocking issue not explicitly flagged by R1 or R3, but it is complementary rather than contradictory. R3 additionally flagged the 21-country replication failures as blocking; R1 treated these as major but not blocking (R1 notes they are from the fragile small-n sample and the failure may reflect SE-type differences). This synthesis treats the replication failures as a major issue (not blocking) on the grounds that the pipeline's own diagnostic flags them as blocking — however, since no re-run of Stage 3 is plausible without new data or SE code, the revision path is quantitative prose/supplementary analysis rather than a notebook re-execution. The Stage 4 re-run (already required for Blocking Issues #1–3) does not affect Stage 3 outputs.
